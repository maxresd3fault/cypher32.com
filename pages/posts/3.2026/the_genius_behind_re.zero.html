The Genius Behind Re:Zero

3/16/2026

"Your insanity is far too sane."

Amalgamation of Genres

I've never been a huge consumer of anime, but every now and then I'll watch some. About 7-8 years ago I watched Re:Zero for the first time, and recently took it upon myself to rewatch the first season. Not only is this the best anime I've watched but one of the best pieces of media I've consumed. In my older years I've come to appreciate the underlying messaging of the show far more than my first watch. If I had to assign a genre I'd call it a "dark fantasy psychological horror thriller existential romance comedy".

This might sound like an impossible mashup, yet the show excels at combing all facets of these genres into a single cohesive work. What really props this franchise up is the amazing story written (and still being written) by Tappei Nagatsuki. The first 25 episode season of the show is based off of nine entire books, yes, nine books.

But what's so great about the story is just how unsuspecting it is. The first few episodes seem rather silly and unserious at times; the viewer might not be expecting that much to come of it in terms of meaningfulness. However, about halfway through things change, they get very dark and more serious. This is a show where the main protagonist goes through a huge transformation with his experiences. In the beginning he is written intentionally to not take things seriously, to be hotheaded and a jerk. But by the end of the show you are truly hooked to all of the characters and feel for them.

Isolation, Immortality & Motivation

A synopsis of Re:Zero boils down to the protagonist, Natsuki Subaru, being suddenly transported out of his boring ordinary life in Japan to a foreign fantasy world. But there's a twist, when he dies he returns to an earlier point in time, preceding any of the events before his death. This is an excellent story engine on multiple levels, it allows for exploration of deep psychological concepts and essentially removes the idea of "plot armor" because anyone, even the main characters, can die. That's not to say that this removes the stakes, because Subaru still feels pain and has to carry all the emotional baggage of dying or watching his loved ones die. But it means you truly don't know who will prevail in a conflict making the show all the more engaging and suspenseful.

Furthermore, the show is an exploration of loneliness and isolation. For reasons unbeknownst to us, Subaru physically cannot tell anyone about his ability to return by death. He has memories of events between him and others that simply never happened in the current timeline he's in, and he can never express that. Other people might not understand why he is acting a certain way or thinks a certain way because in his eyes and their eyes their shared past is completely different. He also has to carry the trauma of being killed, and failing to save his friends from being killed in front of him.

The show has the luxury of showing you how all the main cast react under such immense pressure because when Subaru dies things can go back to "normal" before whatever cataclysmic event or battle. This is one way in which the show manages to be so lighthearted at times but at others explore some of the darkest themes you could imagine.

Subaru is just a normal guy, he has no special powers, he's weak, he's immature, and unaware. Due to all this, his time in this new world is truly a struggle. Part of the development of his character is him finally accepting his flaws himself and admitting it. He is not the same person by the start and end of the show. It's akin to other shows with profound character development such as Walter White from Breaking Bad.

But it's not just Subaru, the main cast are all written exceptionally well. However, at first it doesn't necessarily seem that way. The characters are all written to intentionally put on a face (just as real people do) and as time goes on, through struggle and toil the characters slowly open up and we learn more about them than initially meets the eye.

Another huge message of the show is not giving up. Subaru tries giving up once, and to put it lightly it backfires. The thing is, Subaru physically cannot give up since he can't truly "die". Again, Subaru is weak, he doesn't know much about this new world, he can't do all that much, despite that he doesn't give up and finds ways to put to use what he is good at to help him succeed.

The last main point I want to make before wrapping this up is that the antagonist of the show is simply a mirror of a far more exaggerated Subaru. Subaru understands this to an extent and it is clear it bothers him. It's rather poetic in a way, that his main adversary (someone who he beyond despises), is yet someone so similar to him in many ways.

In Conclusion

Even though Re:Zero is turning 10 years old this year it is certainly worth your time to watch. It really takes story telling to the next level and deals with many profound topics in an interesting and engaging way, while still remaining lighthearted and humorous enough to not become overbearing. I very rarely cry at television, and this is one of the few things that made me tear up while watching it. A huge props to the voice actors for putting on such an incredible performance, not to mention the absolutely amazing orchestral soundtrack. I'm personally very excited to eventually getting around to watching seasons two and three when I have the time.
